1-2. Compare the Life Cycle of Some Common Animals (Helpful and Harmful)

Animals have diverse life cycles that can be categorized as helpful or harmful based on their interactions with humans and the environment.

Example 1: Life Cycle of a Mosquito

Mosquitoes undergo a four-stage life cycle:

  • Egg - Laid on the surface of stagnant water.
  • Larva - Lives in water, feeding on organic matter.
  • Pupa - Transforms into an adult mosquito.
  • Adult - Emerges to reproduce and feed.

Mosquitoes are considered harmful as they spread diseases like malaria and dengue.

Example 2: Life Cycle of a Butterfly

Butterflies have a four-stage life cycle:

  • Egg - Laid on leaves of host plants.
  • Larva (Caterpillar) - Feeds on leaves to grow.
  • Pupa (Chrysalis) - Metamorphoses into an adult.
  • Adult - Emerges to reproduce and pollinate flowers.

Butterflies are beneficial as they aid in plant pollination.

3. Compare the Modes of Reproduction of Some Common Animals

Animals reproduce through various methods. Some reproduce sexually, involving two parents, while others reproduce asexually with a single parent.

Sexual Reproduction: Mammals

Mammals, such as humans and cats, reproduce sexually, where the union of male and female gametes (sperm and egg) creates genetic diversity.

Asexual Reproduction: Hydra

Hydras reproduce asexually through budding, where a small part grows out of the parent and eventually detaches as a new individual.

4. Compare the Characteristics of Vertebrate and Invertebrate Animals

Vertebrates have a backbone and complex organ systems, while invertebrates lack a backbone and may have exoskeletons for structure.

Vertebrates

Vertebrates include mammals, birds, and fish. They have a spine and often complex nervous systems.

Invertebrates

Invertebrates include insects and mollusks. They may have exoskeletons and simpler body structures.

5. Identify Specific Structures of Plants for Adaptation to Different Habitats

Plants have unique adaptations that enable them to thrive in specific habitats, such as deserts or aquatic environments.

Desert Plants: Cactus

The cactus has thick stems for water storage and spines to reduce water loss and deter herbivores.

Aquatic Plants: Water Lily

Water lilies have wide leaves that float on the water's surface to absorb sunlight.

6. Reproduction Through Seeds (Sexual) and Plant Parts (Asexual)

Plants can reproduce in two main ways: sexual reproduction through seeds and asexual reproduction through parts like stems, leaves, and roots.

Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants: Seeds

In flowering plants, sexual reproduction occurs through the formation of seeds. Pollination allows the fertilized egg to develop into a seed, which grows into a new plant under suitable conditions.

Asexual Reproduction: Vegetative Propagation

Plants like potatoes and strawberries reproduce asexually by growing new plants from parts of the original plant, such as stems, roots, or leaves.

7. Techniques for Plant Propagation (e.g., Stem Cuttings, Marcotting, Grafting, Leaf Cutting)

People have devised various ways or techniques to propagate plants from parts of other plants. This allows for quicker growth and the production of new plants without needing to start from seeds.

Stem Cuttings

In stem cutting, a portion of a plant's stem is cut off and planted in soil or water to grow a new plant. This is common with plants like roses, geraniums, and lavender. The cutting will develop roots, and a new plant will emerge.

Marcotting (Air Layering)

Marcotting involves inducing root growth on a branch while it is still attached to the parent plant. The branch is wounded, covered with moist soil or moss, and wrapped in plastic to keep it humid. Once roots form, the branch is cut off and planted separately.

Grafting

Grafting involves joining two plant parts together to form a new plant. One part (the scion) is taken from a desired plant and attached to another part (the rootstock), which is often hardier. This is commonly done in fruit trees like apples and citrus to combine desirable traits such as fruit production and disease resistance.

Leaf Cutting

Leaf cutting is when a healthy leaf is cut from a plant and used to grow a new plant. This method is often used with succulents like jade plants or African violets. The leaf, when placed in soil, develops roots and a new plant begins to grow from it.
